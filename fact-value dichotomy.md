Hume's "guillotine" is often tr eated in contemporary philosophy as establishing a strict barrier between facts and values. This is a massive voerstatement. Properly understood, Hume's claim is that a purely descriptive set of premises cannot yield a normative conclusion unless at least one normative premise is already present.

> In every system of morality, which I have hitherto met with, I have always remarked, that the author proceeds for some time in the ordinary ways of reasoning, and establishes the being of a God, or the order of the universe, or the particular qualities of men; when of a sudden I find, that these facts he derives a prescriptive, of what ought to be done... This change is imperceptible; but is, however, of the last consequence. For as this ought, or ought not, expresses some new relation or affirmation, it is necessary that it should be observed or explained; and at present I do not find it [^1]

As we can observe here, Hume himself did not establish a categorical, absolute barrier between facts and values. He only noted that the many moral arguments implicitly introduce a prescriptive assumption without making it explicit. The "guillotine" is a misreading, and a simplification, exploited by subjectivists or relativists to claim that normativity has no rational basis. Hume's claim is true in and of itself, but is philosophically irrelevant. The issue is that the concept of value arises only in relation to life. 

> The Objectivist ethics holds man’s life as the standard of value—and his own life as the ethical purpose of every individual man... Man must choose his actions, values and goals by the standard of that which is proper to man—in order to achieve, maintain, fulfill and enjoy that ultimate value, that end in itself, which is his own life. [^2]

Further grounding this point, Rand explains that "It is only the concept of 'Life' that makes the concept of 'Value' possible ... It is only to a living entity that things can be good or evil"[^2]. A living organism faces the constant alternative of existence or nonexistence, and therefore must act to maintain itself. Consequently, the fact–value dichotomy cannot be as absolute. The idea that we start with a purely descriptive base and then derive normativity later is a category error. To ask what follows already presupposes that one ought to follow reasons rather than ignore them. Values arise from the fact that a living being must act to sustain and advance its life. To act is to choose, and to choose is to identify which states of existence further one's life and which undermine it.

Choosing X over Y is to treat X as what ought to be done. If A treats X as a chosen end, and then systematically acts to make ¬X true, then A is undermining A's own agency; A's actions negate the content of their own choice. These values are not arbitrary. 

> Man has been called a rational being, but rationality is a matter of choice—and the alternative his nature offers him is: rational being or suicidal animal. Man has to be man—by choice; he has to hold his life as a value—by choice; he has to learn to sustain it—by choice; he has to discover the values it requires and practice his virtues—by choice. A code of values accepted by choice is a code of morality. [^2]

That is to say, every human being, as a rational agent, must act in ways that sustain and promote his life, for life is the standard of value and reason is man's only means of knowledge and action. In human beings, those actions depend on cognition: we must identify the facts of reality in order to survive. One cannot reject reason without simultaneously undermining the conditions that make action possible. A person may claim that life is not a value, but if he attempts to live, he is already guided by the requirements of life. Whether he acknowledges it or not, reality—not whim—determines which actions sustain life and which destroy it.

Some object that the injunction to choose life is itself an arbitrary starting point, or that grounding ethics in the choice to live makes morality arbitrary. But the alternative of life or death is a basic fact of living organisms. The choice is not a theoretical premise posited into ethical reason, it is the basic condition that gives rise to ethics in the first place. Moral principles identify the actions required for human life. A category "ought" detached from the nature and conditions of agency is incoherent. The most basic normativity is implicit in choosing as such.

Facts about reality determine which actions sustain human life and which undermine it. Consider a simple example: sunlight exists, and within certain limits it is beneficial to human life. Given the inescapable valuation of continued agency, this fact implies that humans ought to seek sunlight for agriculture of health and avoid overexposure. Evaluations of truth are therefore inseparable from value judgements. Because human survival depends on identifying reality correctly, adherence to truth is a necessity for a rational being, and a deliberate evasion of reality constitutes a failure to live by the requirements of rational human life.

Honest mistakes differ from deliberate evasion of reality, as seen in intellectual movements that consciously propagate falsehoods; the former may be corrected, while the latter is morally culpable. Honest error merely reflects epistemic limitation and is corrigible through further inquiry. Man's adherence to reality is a moral imperative. To consciously evade facts is to contradict the faculty that makes reason possible. Reason is man's only means of knowledge, and to deliberately reject it is to commit a moral error.

[^2]: Ayn Rand, The Virtue of Selfishness: A New Concept of Egoism (New York: Signet, 1964), chapter "The Objectivist Ethics"
