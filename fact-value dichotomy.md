Hume's "guillotine" is often treated in contemporary philosophy as establishing a strict barrier between facts and values. This is a massive overstatement. Properly understood, Hume's claim is that a purely descriptive set of premises cannot yield a normative conclusion unless at least one normative premise is already present.

> In every system of morality, which I have hitherto met with, I have always remarked, that the author proceeds for some time in the ordinary ways of reasoning, and establishes the being of a God, or the order of the universe, or the particular qualities of men; when of a sudden I find, that these facts he derives a prescription, of what ought to be done... This change is imperceptible; but is, however, of the last consequence. For as this ought, or ought not, expresses some new relation or affirmation, it is necessary that it should be observed or explained; and at present I do not find it [^1]

As we can observe here, Hume himself did not establish a categorical, absolute barrier between facts and values. He only noted that the many moral arguments implicitly introduce a prescriptive assumption without making it explicit. The "guillotine" is a misreading and a simplification. Nevertheless, it is often interpreted as implying that normativity cannot be grounded in facts about reality. Hume's claim is true in itself, but does not undermine the possibility of objective ethics.

> The Objectivist ethics holds man’s life as the standard of value—and his own life as the ethical purpose of every individual man... Man must choose his actions, values and goals by the standard of that which is proper to man—in order to achieve, maintain, fulfill and enjoy that ultimate value, that end in itself, which is his own life. [^2]

Further grounding this point, Rand explains that "It is only the concept of 'Life' that makes the concept of 'Value' possible ... It is only to a living entity that things can be good or evil"[^2]. Values arise because a living entity faces the alternative of existence or nonexistence and must act in order to remain alive. In the case of humans, this introduces a conditional normativity: if one chooses to live, then certain actions are objectively required. The idea that normativity is added to an otherwise value-free description of reality is a category error. If a human being chooses to live, he thereby commits himself to the requirements that life imposes.

Choosing X over Y is to treat X as a value. If an agent treats X as a chosen end, and then consistently acts in ways that negate it, then he is undermining his own agency; his actions negate the content of their own choice.

> Man has been called a rational being, but rationality is a matter of choice—and the alternative his nature offers him is: rational being or suicidal animal. Man has to be man—by choice; he has to hold his life as a value—by choice; he has to learn to sustain it—by choice; he has to discover the values it requires and practice his virtues—by choice. A code of values accepted by choice is a code of morality. [^2]

That is, every human being, as a rational agent, must act in ways that sustain and promote his life, for life is the standard of value and reason is man's only means of knowledge and action. In human beings, those actions depend on cognition: we must identify the facts of reality in order to survive. One cannot reject reason without simultaneously undermining the conditions that make action possible. A person may claim that life is not a value. Yet if he attempts to live, his actions must conform to the requirements of life. Whether he acknowledges it or not, reality—not whim—determines which actions sustain life and which destroy it.

Some object that the injunction to choose life is itself an arbitrary starting point, or that grounding ethics in the choice to live makes morality arbitrary. But the alternative of life or death is a basic fact of living organisms. The choice is not a theoretical premise inserted into ethical reason, it is the basic condition that gives rise to ethics in the first place. Moral principles identify the actions required for human life. A category "ought" detached from the nature and conditions of agency is incoherent. Because action presupposes the pursuit of some end, the act of choosing already presupposes the framework within which normative evaluation becomes possible.

The requirements of human life are determined by the facts of reality, which determine the actions that sustain human life. Because human survival depends on identifying those facts correctly, adherence to truth becomes a practical value for a rational being. Evaluations of truth are therefore inseparable from value judgements. To evade reality is to undermine the very conditions that make successful action possible.

Honest error merely reflects epistemic limitation and is corrigible through further inquiry, whereas the deliberate propagation of falsehoods is morally culpable. Man's adherence to reality is a moral imperative. To consciously evade facts is to contradict the faculty that makes reason possible. Reason is man's only means of knowledge, and to deliberately reject it is to commit a moral error.

[^1]: Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature, Book III, Part I, Section 1 (1739-1740)
[^2]: Ayn Rand, The Virtue of Selfishness: A New Concept of Egoism (New York: Signet, 1964), chapter "The Objectivist Ethics"
