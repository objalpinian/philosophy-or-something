Hume's guillotine is treated as this decisive barrier between facts and values despite Hume explicitly denying that because subjectivists are massive fucking retards. Properly understood, Hume's point is that a purely descriptive set a premises cannot yield a normative conclusion without at least one normative premise. This is true in and of itself, but is philosophically irrelevant. The issue is that the concept of value arises only in relation to life. A living organism faces the constant alternative of existence or nonexistence, and therefore must act to maintain itself. Consequently, the fact–value dichotomy cannot be as absolute. The idea that we start with a purely descriptive base and then derive normativity later is a category error. To ask what follows already presupposes that one ought to follow reasons rather than ignore them. There is no point outside normativity from which we later "add" value.

Choosing X over Y is to treat X as what ought to be done. These values are not arbitrary, every living organism must act in ways that sustain its life. In human beings, those actions depend on cognition: we must identify the facts of reality in order to survive. To act is to treat continued functioning as conditionally preferable to immediate non existence. To perform any action at time t, an agent must implicitly treat continued existence  and the continued functioning of the relevant capacities as preferable to non existence long enough to complete the action. This valuation is not chosen because it is implicit in action itself. One can reject it only by ceasing to act altogether, which must be carried out through action and thus presuppose the very condition they reject. A person may claim that life is not a value, but if he attempts to live he must still perform the actions on which life depends. Whether he acknowledges it or not, reality determines which actions sustain life and which destroy it.

Some subjectivists object that the injunction to choose life is itself an arbitrary starting point, or that grounding ethics in the choice to live makes morality arbitrary. But the alternative of life or death is a basic fact of living organisms. The choice is not a theoretical premise posited into ethical reason, it is the basic condition that gives rise to ethics in the first place. Moral principles identify the actions requires for human life. A category "ought" detached from the nature and conditions of agency is incoherent. The most basic normativity is implicit in choosing as such.

Facts about reality determine which actions sustain human life and which undermine it. Consider a simple example: sunlight exists, and within certain limits it is beneficial to human life. Given the inescapable valuation of continued agency, this fact implies that humans ought seek sunlight for agriculture of health and avoid overexposure. Evaluations of truth are therefore inseparable from value judgements. Because human survival depends on identifying reality correctly, adherence to truth is a necessity for a rational being, and a deliberate evasion of reality constitutes a failure to live by the requirements of rational human life.

Honest mistakes differ from deliberate evasion of reality, as seen in intellectual movements that consciously propagate falsehoods; the former may be corrected, while the latter is morally culpable. Honest error merely reflects epistemic limitation and is corrigible through further inquiry. Deliberate evasion, by contrast, is a willful refusal to integrate evidence because it threatens prior commitments. Such evasion undermines agency itself, since effective action depends on accurate representations of reality. To that extent, truth is normatively binding for agents, and its deliberate rejection constitutes a moral failure.
